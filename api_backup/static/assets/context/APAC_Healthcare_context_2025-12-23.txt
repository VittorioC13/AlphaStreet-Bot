Asian and European stock markets traded mostly lower on Tuesday as investors pulled back ahead of crucial U.S. employment and inflation data. Weakness in technology stocks led the decline, while caution grew over the future path of global interest rates.U.S. stock futures pointed lower early Tuesday. S&P 500 futures fell 0.5%. Dow Jones Industrial Average futures slipped 0.3%. Investors are positioning ahead of economic reports that could influence the Federal Reserve’s next policy move.Stocks showed sharp divergence in early trading, with speculative names leading gains while mega-cap tech traded mixed. Biodexa Pharmaceuticals surged nearly 60% to $7.63 on heavy volume, making it one of the session’s top performers. AMC Robotics jumped 35% to $9.83, extending its recent momentum, while B. Riley Financial climbed almost 28% to $4.75 amid renewed buying interest.Smaller moves were seen in Presidio Property Trust, up modestly at $4.04, and BitMine Immersion Technologies, which added just over 1% to $31.29. Intel edged higher by 1.2% to $37.95, offering some support to the chip sector.On the downside, Nvidia slipped 0.4% to $175.52 after recent volatility in AI-linked stocks. Tesla fell nearly 1% to $470.87 as traders locked in profits near record levels. BP declined 1.4% alongside weaker oil prices, while IREN also edged lower. Overall, trading reflected selective risk-taking rather than a broad market move.Japan’s Nikkei 225 dropped sharply, falling 1.6% to 49,383.29. The decline followed fresh factory data showing manufacturing activity remains under pressure. The S&P Global Flash PMI rose to 49.7 in December from 48.7 in November, still below the 50 level that signals expansion.Markets are closely watching Japan ahead of the Bank of Japan’s policy meeting on Friday. Investors widely expect an interest rate hike. Any shift by the BOJ could ripple through global bond markets, currencies, and even cryptocurrencies.Chinese stocks also moved lower after disappointing November economic figures. Retail sales rose just 1.3% year over year, the slowest pace since 2022. Lending and investment activity weakened as well, raising concerns about growth momentum heading into year-end.Hong Kong’s Hang Seng Index fell 1.5% to 25,235.41. The Shanghai Composite dropped 1.1% to 3,824.81. Analysts said the data points to slowing growth, with expectations that China’s economy may expand at around 4% in the fourth quarter.Technology shares led losses across parts of Asia. South Korea’s Kospi sank 2.2% to 3,999.13. Chipmakers were hit hard. SK Hynix slid 4.3%, while Samsung Electronics fell 1.9%.Taiwan’s Taiex declined 1.2%. Australia’s S&P/ASX 200 eased 0.4% to 8,598.90, reflecting broader regional caution.European markets were mixed. Germany’s DAX fell 0.4% to 24,142.20. France’s CAC 40 edged 0.1% higher to 8,129.43. Britain’s FTSE 100 slipped 0.3% to 9,722.23.On Wall Street, stocks closed lower Monday. The S&P 500 slipped 0.2%. The Dow dipped 0.1%. The Nasdaq fell 0.6%, weighed down by tech and AI-related stocks. Oracle dropped another 2.7%, while Broadcom sank 5.6%. Nvidia edged up 0.7%.Oil prices also moved lower. U.S. crude fell $1.08 to $55.74 a barrel. Brent crude dropped $1.06 to $59.50.The main focus now shifts to U.S. economic data. Economists expect Tuesday’s jobs report to show a net gain of about 40,000 jobs, with unemployment holding near 4.4%. Inflation data due Thursday is forecast to show consumer prices rising 3.1% year over year.Investors hope for cooling labor conditions that could allow the Federal Reserve to cut rates, without pushing the economy into recession. The balance remains delicate, and markets are bracing for volatility.
